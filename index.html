Introduction

The universe is shrouded in many mysteries, one such of these is the inexorable passage of time. It is principle to our reality and abstruse. We began by looking to the heavens, observing the sun (and other celestial bodies alike), it is from this that we define a day and night, a year being the period of the earth’s orbit around the sun which is about 365 days. Counting each day by grouping them into weeks and months would give rise to the calendar. Among the earliest non-astronomical devices for measuring time was the water clock dating back to 2000 B.C. (Major, 2007). By the 14th century large mechanical clocks making use of weight then springs were developed. However, the problems with these designs which were also the most critical part their poor regulating system (a mechanical system capable of inherently stable periodic motion). Thus, the history of the advancement in mechanical clockmaking is the history of the development of this part of clock design. By the 17th century pendulum clocks credited to Christiaan Huygens with later refinements reduced his clock’s error to less than 10 seconds a day (Suplee, 2009). By the 1920s a new system of regulators known as a quartz crystal oscillator improved the accuracy of and miniaturisation of clocks. By far the most advanced way of telling the time in recent years by use of an atomic clocks, as far as we know atoms provide the most stable regulators there could possibly be in the universe. This paper will begin by recounting the history of the science behind atoms, the design of these clocks, and their applications in modern day.
